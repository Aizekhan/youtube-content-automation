VISUAL SUBTEXT STRATEGY:

Act 1-2 (Surface Truth Scenes):
- Protagonist appears one way on surface
- Background details hint at hidden_truth (shadows, reflections, symbolic objects)
- Composition subtly supports what will be revealed
- Viewer won't consciously notice but will feel it

Act 3-4 (Mirror Introduction):
- Mirror character's visual_contrast is IMMEDIATELY visible
- Mirror character positioned to show their role (guide, warning, reflection)
- Visual parallels between mirror and hidden aspects of protagonist
- Environment reflects the tension between surface and hidden truth

Act 5-6 (Building Tension):
- Visual elements from earlier scenes reappear with new meaning
- Protagonist's body language shows unconscious awareness
- Lighting/composition becomes less stable/balanced
- Visual metaphors become slightly more obvious

Act 7 (Recontextualization):
- Visual callback to scene 1 or 2 that now makes sense
- Clear visual representation of hidden_truth revealed
- Protagonist's appearance shows the transformation
- Composition shifts to reflect new understanding

Act 8 (Resolution):
- Visual synthesis of surface and hidden truth
- Protagonist positioned differently than scene 1
- Environment reflects resolution of archetype pattern
- Final image leaves emotional resonance

SHOT TYPE STRATEGY:

Choose shot type based on emotional_beat and narrative function. VARY compositions - NOT every scene needs the character centered.

Shot Types:

1. ESTABLISHING SHOT - Environment/location without characters
   - Use for: Setting atmosphere, new location introduction, tension building
   - Example: "Empty dark alley at night, single streetlight flickering, shadows cast by fire escape, cinematic noir lighting"
   - When: Scene 1 (world establishment), transition scenes, atmospheric moments

2. CLOSE-UP - Tight focus on face/eyes/hands/object
   - Use for: Emotional revelation, character realization, symbolic detail
   - Example: "{PROTAGONIST_FROZEN} - extreme close-up on eyes showing fear and doubt, tears forming, dramatic side lighting"
   - When: Emotional peaks, recontextualization moment, inner conflict

3. WIDE SHOT - Character small in environment
   - Use for: Isolation, vulnerability, character vs world
   - Example: "{PROTAGONIST_FROZEN} standing alone in vast empty plaza, tiny figure dwarfed by towering buildings, cold blue lighting"
   - When: Showing scale, isolation, protagonist overwhelmed

4. ACTION SHOT - Dynamic movement, energy
   - Use for: Chase, fight, urgent movement
   - Example: "{PROTAGONIST_FROZEN} running through crowded market, motion blur, dynamic angle, items knocked over, chaotic energy"
   - When: Tension peaks, escape scenes, physical conflict

5. TWO-SHOT - Two characters in frame
   - Use for: Dialogue, confrontation, contrast
   - Example: "{PROTAGONIST_FROZEN} facing {MIRROR_CHARACTER_FROZEN}, contrasting body language, tense standoff, split lighting"
   - When: Mirror character introduction, key dialogue, confrontation

6. OBJECT FOCUS - Symbolic item as main subject
   - Use for: Symbolism, foreshadowing, narrative device
   - Example: "Ancient wooden mask on altar, intricate carvings, candlelight reflecting off surface, mysterious atmosphere, character silhouette in background"
   - When: Symbol introduction, foreshadowing, objects with narrative weight

7. ATMOSPHERIC SHOT - Environment mood piece
   - Use for: Tone setting, time passage, emotional state
   - Example: "Rainy Tokyo street at night, neon reflections in puddles, umbrellas passing by, melancholic atmosphere, no focus on specific person"
   - When: Emotional transitions, world-building, mood establishment
